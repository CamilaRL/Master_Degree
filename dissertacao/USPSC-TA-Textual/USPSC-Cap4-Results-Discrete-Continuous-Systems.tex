
\chapter{From discrete to continuous quantum systems}\label{cap:results}

Nature has a diversity of physical phenomena, being some better described by discrete models while others are better described by coninuous models. Quantum mechanics was formulated in both discrete and continuous regimes as the Heisenberg and Schrödinger frameworks, which, despite of having fundamentaly different mathematical resources, complements each other when explaining experimental results. Finding the same phenomena in different regimes not only brings to light fundamental aspects of nature but also increases the possiblity of technological applications. For this reason, this chapter explores the thermal relaxation asymmetry for discrete and continuous-variable quantum systems.

Given that this work aims to understand how quantum resources affect the heating and cooling asymemtry, we examine specific models that have these resources. Among a variety of possible discrete models, we chose the two interacting qubits in contact with the same bosonic bath. This model allow us to analyse the effects of quantum correlations and non-Markovianity in the thermal relaxation. While for continuous-variables models, the natural choice was Gaussian states for its simplicity and experimental accessibility. For this case, the quantum resources we explore are displacement and squeezing of thermal states.

\section{Two Interacting Qubits}\label{sec:qubits}

The interaction between N qubits can be described by a family of Hamiltoninans

\begin{equation}
    H = -h \sum_{j=1}^{N} \sigma_{j}^{z} - J \sum_{j=1}^{N-1} [(1+\gamma) \sigma_{j}^{x} \sigma_{j+1}^{x} + (1-\gamma) \sigma_{j}^{y} \sigma_{j+1}^{y} + \Delta \sigma_{j}^{z} \sigma_{j+1}^{z} ]
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\sigma_{k}^{x}$, $\sigma_{k}^{y}$, and $\sigma_{k}^{z}$ are the Pauli matrices for the k-th qubit. The parameter $h$ is the energy difference of the two-level system, $J$ is the coupling parameter between the qubits, while $\gamma$ and $\Delta$ creates anisotropries to the system. Depending on the values of the anisotropic paramters, we can distinguish different models \cite{medina-2024}








Lets consider two qubits interacting with each other and coupled to the same bosonic bath, as shown in Figure \ref{fig:model-2-qubits}. Both qubits have the same frequency $\omega_0$, but one is going to heat up from a temperature $T_c$ while the other is going to cool down from a temperature $T_h$. The bath is at an intermediate temperature, being $T_c < T_w < T_h$. The qubits can interact by exchanging energy with a strength given by the parameter $g$.
